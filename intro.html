Before you begin

"Let me tell you the thing nobody said to me when I was your age.
Nobody pulled me aside. Nobody sat me down over coffee and said — hey, before you walk into the next ten years of your life, there is something you should know. The people who could have said it were still too deep in it themselves to see it clearly. So they said nothing. And I walked in blind. You are not walking in blind. Not today."

Six sessions. Tony's story — the architecture, the cost, the turn, and why he built this for you. Listen in order. There's no better place to start.

Session 01 of 06

The Architecture

Why your 20s are structurally, not personally, built around alcohol. The design nobody chose and nobody questioned.

Listened
The Architecture — Why your 20s are the hardest decade ~6 min
0:00 —

Your 20s — and I mean this literally, not as a feeling, not as a metaphor — your 20s are structurally, architecturally, in the actual social calendar of your life, built around alcohol. The Friday night invite. The first date where she orders wine before you've even properly sat down and looks at you like the next move is yours. The work event where the bar is the only room where anyone actually relaxes and talks like a human being.

This is not your weakness. This is the design. Nobody built it to hurt you. It just is what it is. But knowing that — really knowing that — changes something. Because for thirty years I did not know that. I thought it was me.

I'll give you one specific memory from my early 20s. Not dramatic. Embarrassingly ordinary. I was 22, living in a shared house with three other guys. One Wednesday night — not a weekend, Wednesday — I was bored. Not sad. Not anxious. Just bored. And I remember opening the fridge, seeing a half-empty bottle of cheap vodka that belonged to my roommate, and thinking, "He won't notice if I take one sip." So I took one sip. Then another. Then I stood in the kitchen, alone, at 10pm on a Wednesday, drinking warm vodka from the bottle, and I thought — I genuinely thought — "This is what interesting people do. This is texture. This is life."

I was so proud of myself for being spontaneous. Looking back: I was a 22-year-old man standing in semi-darkness drinking something that tasted like hand sanitizer, congratulating himself for having a personality. The lack of self-awareness is almost beautiful.

That was the quietest, stupidest beginning of something that would take me thirty years to notice.

Session 02 of 06

The Cost

Not a list. One specific thing. The slow, quiet work alcohol does before you notice it's happened.

Listened
The Cost — What thirty years quietly took ~6 min
0:00 —

One real thing alcohol cost me. Not a list. One thing. I had a best friend in my late twenties — a guy named Dan. We had been close since college. And at some point, I stopped being someone he could rely on. Not because I was mean. Because I was never fully there.

He told me once, laughing but not really laughing, "You're like a screensaver. You're still there on the screen, but you're not doing anything." I laughed too. I thought it was a joke. Three years later, he moved to another city, and we never had the kind of friendship again where you call each other at 2am when something real happens.

I lost him slowly, not in a fight, just in a thousand small absences. That was alcohol's work. Quiet. Efficient. And I didn't even notice until he was gone.

And somewhere in all of that, a version of myself I never got to meet — a person who was in there the whole time, wondering what the other timeline felt like. What the mornings felt like. What it was like to actually choose your life instead of drift through it.

I am not telling you that to make you feel heavy. I am telling you because you are not me. You are here, at your age, asking a different question than I ever knew how to ask. And that single quiet difference is genuinely everything.

Session 03 of 06

The Turn

No rock bottom. No intervention. Just a boring, exhausted realisation — and what happened the morning after.

Listened
The Turn — The night I put the beer down ~5 min
0:00 —

I was 37, sitting on my couch, watching a show I didn't care about, drinking a beer I didn't want. And I had this thought — not dramatic, not profound — "I have done this exact thing ten thousand times. And I have never once woken up the next day and thought, I am so glad I had that beer."

That was it. That was the turn. No rock bottom. No intervention. Just a boring, exhausted realization that I had been running a cost-benefit analysis in my head for two decades and every single time the answer was the same, and I had just been ignoring it.

I put the beer down. I went to bed. The next day I didn't drink. And then the day after that.

I finally saw clearly that I was not drinking because I loved it. I was drinking because I was afraid of being the person who didn't drink. Afraid of being boring. Afraid of being the one holding sparkling water while everyone else held something that looked like fun.

And then one night I went to a party, said no to a drink, and nothing happened. No one cared. No one even noticed. I went home, slept well, woke up without a headache, and thought, "Oh. That was the whole monster? A paper tiger?"

I had been running from a ghost. And the ghost was me.

Session 04 of 06

What This Is Actually For

This course wasn't built for people who've lost enough. It was built for the person standing at a house party at 11pm, holding sparkling water, smiling like they're fine.

Listened
What This Is Actually For — The Saturday night nobody wrote about ~5 min
0:00 —

Here is what the standard sobriety conversation gets wrong. It talks about rock bottom like it is an entry requirement. Like you need to have lost enough — the job, the marriage, something specific and humiliating — before you are allowed to want things to be different.

You may not have a rock bottom. What you have is a Saturday night.

Picture this. It is 11pm. Your friends are at a birthday party. And you are standing in a room full of people who are three drinks in and getting louder, and someone hands you something and you say no thanks and they say come on just one and you say no really and they say — and this is the part that gets you — you are so boring now. And they are laughing when they say it. But they mean it a little. And you know they mean it a little.

And you are standing there holding your sparkling water, smiling like a person who is completely fine, while something in your chest is doing something you do not have a word for yet.

Nobody wrote a course about that Saturday night. Nobody sat down and said — that feeling you are carrying home at 11pm, let's talk about that specifically. I am talking about it.

Session 05 of 06

What Makes This Different

The community. The live sessions. The people who lived their 20s at the bottom of a bottle — and come back to tell you what it actually cost.

Listened
What Makes This Different — The community is not a bonus feature ~6 min
0:00 —

The community is not a bonus feature. It is not the place you go after you finish the real course. It is the thing that makes the rest of it stick.

Here is what nobody tells you about getting sober in your 20s. The loneliness is not the kind you expect. You might have plenty of friends. You might have people around you all the time. But you are doing something none of them are doing. And there is a particular kind of lonely that comes from being surrounded by people who are on your side and still feeling like nobody in the room is speaking your language.

The community fixes that. People posting wins every day — not the big dramatic ones, the small ones, the ones that cost everything and look like nothing from the outside.

And live sessions — not just me. People who lived their entire 20s buried in the bottle. In the fun way. The everyone-loves-them-at-parties way. Who are sitting now in their 30s and 40s trying to work out what it quietly cost them — and coming to talk to you about it. Not to scare you. To tell you the truth about what they wish they had known, from inside a life where they did not know it.

That is not something you can manufacture. When it lands, it lands somewhere that stays.

Session 06 of 06

One Sentence to My 22-Year-Old Self

The truth with the laugh in it. Because that's what being 22 actually deserves.

Listened
One Sentence — The truth with the laugh in it ~4 min
0:00 —

Here is the one sentence I would actually say to my 22-year-old self. Not polished. Not motivational. Just true.

I would lean across the table and say: "You are going to spend the next fifteen years trying to solve a loneliness problem with a chemistry problem, and it will never work — not because you're weak, but because you're solving the wrong equation."

And then I would add, because I can't help myself: "Also, that thing you're about to say to her? Don't. Just trust me. Let her order first. And for God's sake, stop trying to make 'I'm a chaos agent' your brand. You're not a chaos agent. You're just tired."

That's the truth with the laugh in it, because that's what being 22 actually deserves — honesty and a little bit of a joke at your own expense.

I built what I needed at your age. It is here. It is waiting.
