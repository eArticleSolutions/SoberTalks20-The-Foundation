Module 00 · Orientation

"This is your orientation. Your compass into everything that follows.

Before the modules, before the tools, before any of the practical stuff — you need to know exactly what you walked into. Not a fix. Not a 30-day reset. Not a system that works on the good days and quietly disappears on the hard ones.

A framework. Built specifically for the social architecture of being sober in your 20s. The rooms that used to run on autopilot. The scripts that no longer exist. The version of yourself you are still figuring out how to introduce.

You are not broken. You are not recovering from something shameful. You are building something. Hold onto that."

Six sessions. An honest frame, a path matched to where you are right now, and the infrastructure that makes everything else stick. Your path through them depends on one question. Watch in order. There is no better place to start.

What This Course Is — and Isn't ~6 min
0:00 —

Two mistakes people make arriving here: the first is expecting a fix — the final answer that makes it all click instantly. When it does not, they close the tab and add it to the list of things that did not work. The second is arriving with one eye already on the exit, treating it like background content. Both postures waste your time. And your time — specifically your 20s, specifically right now — is the one thing in this that is genuinely, irreversibly limited.

This is a practical framework. Built specifically for the social architecture of being sober in your 20s — the rooms that alcohol used to smooth over. The Friday night invite. The first date. The work event where the bar is the only room where anyone relaxes. We go through those rooms with actual tools and actual sentences. Not the version where someone tells you to be strong and leaves you standing in a car park with no script.

What this is not: not therapy, not a 12-step programme, not a 30-day fix. And not motivation. Motivation shows up on the good days. It does not show up on the hard ones. The tools here are built for precisely when you do not feel good. That is the entire point.

You will not be called an alcoholic in this course. You will not be asked to label yourself or declare a rock bottom. The frame is this: you are an intelligent adult making a specific, high-leverage choice about how to build your 20s. You are not broken. You are building something. Hold onto that — it is more durable than any of the alternatives.

The physical part may be getting easier. Or it may not be yet — and either way, that is fine, that is normal. But the social part is relentless. It does not take weekends off. It does not care that you are tired. Every Friday is a test. Every group chat is a reminder of somewhere you are going and dreading, or somewhere you are not going and quietly grieving. And both of those — going and not going — can feel equally hard on the same weekend.

The hard part is not the craving in the way people describe it. The hard part is script failure. What do you say when someone hands you a drink and will not take no the first time? What do you do with your hands at a party when everyone else is holding something? (This sounds trivial. It is not trivial. Nobody talks about the hands.) How do you leave early without it becoming a whole conversation about why?

The social behaviours that alcohol made automatic are now manual. And manual is exhausting in a way that is genuinely hard to explain to someone who has not been there. You are not weak. You are doing by hand something that used to run on its own. That is not a character problem. That is a tools problem. And tools problems have solutions.

Right now, this Friday, you need sentences. Scripts that work in real situations with real people in the real rooms you are already having to walk into. Module 02 is built for exactly that. Start with Module 01 — it names the architecture you are working against — then straight to Module 02. The identity work is real and it matters enormously. It is just the work of month two and month three. Right now you need sentences before you need philosophy.

You have the days. You did the hard part — whatever version of hard the early weeks were for you. The physical withdrawal. The awkward phase of telling people and watching them process it in real time, which is its own particular kind of exhausting — being the support act in your own difficult conversation. The weekends of saying no, again and again. You got through all of it. You are still here. That means something real, and it deserves to be said before anything else.

But somewhere around month three or four, something shifted that you did not expect. Not the sobriety — the sobriety held. The life still felt wrong. Like you built something and it still did not fit quite right. Like you won the argument but somehow lost the room. The friend group changed in ways you did not fully anticipate and cannot explain without it sounding like a complaint you are not entitled to make.

What you are feeling is not a willpower problem. You have already proved your willpower. The days exist. What it is — and this is the thing that changes everything once you can name it — is an identity gap. Alcohol was doing an identity job. It was currency in rooms. A personality shortcut. A way of walking in somewhere and signalling, without a word, that you are fun and easy to be around. When you stopped, you stopped that function. But the gap it left — you have not fully filled it yet.

This is not a character problem. It is a gap in the architecture — specific, knowable, fixable. The values work. The identity rebuild. The social blueprint built on purpose rather than inherited from a drinking life. Module 03 is where this starts. That is where you are going.

A lapse is not a verdict. It is not evidence that you are the kind of person who cannot do this. It is not a return to zero — and the idea that it is a return to zero, that you have lost everything you built, is one of the most damaging things about how this conversation usually gets framed. Because it turns a gap in the architecture into a story about your character. Those are completely, fundamentally different things.

A lapse is information. Specific information about a specific trigger, a specific moment, a specific gap in the plan that you now know about — because it showed itself. That is not failure. That is the process working exactly as it is supposed to.

Here is what is actually hard about where you are right now. It is not the sobriety itself — you know you can be sober, you have done it. What is hard is the voice. The one that has decided this moment means something permanent about your character. That voice is not telling you the truth. It is telling you a story it built quickly out of one night's worth of evidence, and it would like you to believe that story permanently. Do not let it run the narrative.

You do not start over after a lapse. You continue — with more information than you had before. Module 12 is where you are going first. Not because the protocol is the most important thing in this course, but because having it built before you need it next time is the single most useful thing you can do today, in this calm moment, before the next hard night arrives. Go to Module 12.

Your First 7 Days — The foundation before everything else ~6 min
0:00 —

Seven days. Seven things. One per day. And before you decide these are suggestions — they are not. The first seven days set the pattern for everything that follows. Not the first thirty, not the first ninety. The first seven. Because they determine whether this becomes part of the actual structure of your life, or another thing you meant to come back to and didn't.

Day one. Read the Module 01 manifesto. All of it. Until the core idea actually lands — that your 20s are structurally built around alcohol, and that is not your weakness, that is the design. Read it until it feels true, not just logical. You will know when you get there.

Day two. Post one sentence in the introductions channel. Name, where you are in the journey, one thing you are working on. The sentence does not need to be good. The act of showing up publicly — saying this out loud somewhere outside your own head — matters more than the words. It just needs to exist.

Day three. Watch Module 02 and pick the deflection line that sounds most like your voice. Not the one that sounds best on paper — the one that sounds like you. Memorise it before your next social event. You do not need a speech. You need one sentence that comes out without you having to think about it in the moment.

Day four. Name one upcoming social event you are already quietly dreading. Write it down somewhere. Unnamed things have a way of growing in the dark — getting heavier the longer they stay vague. Name it and it gets smaller.

Day five. Post in the sober buddy matching channel. Three minutes. The highest return action of your entire first week. Feeling like it is not a requirement. Doing it is.

Day six. Open Module 06 and find the Late Night Audio. Listen now, before you need it. Know where it lives before 11pm on a hard Saturday — not searching for it while you are already in the middle of one.

Day seven. Write your first draft identity statement. I am someone who — and then finish the sentence with something true. Do not edit it. Do not wait for the right words — the right words come out of the wrong ones, not before them. The foundation before everything else.

Your Community — How It Actually Works ~7 min
0:00 —

The community is not a bonus feature. It is not the place you go after you finish the real course to celebrate and feel good briefly. It is the infrastructure that makes the rest of it stick. If you skip it, you are doing this without the part that actually works.

Here is what the research on long-term behaviour change says consistently, across decades of studies in every context: it is not the content that changes behaviour. It is the connection. Information held alone, on a phone at midnight — mostly does not change behaviour. Information held inside a relationship, where someone else knows what you are trying to do, where someone else is inside the same week you are inside — that changes behaviour. Every time. That is not an opinion. That is what the data says.

Six channels, each with a specific purpose. Welcome — read the pinned post before anything else. Introductions — your first post, something true, that is always enough. Daily Wins — small things only; the wins that cost everything and get the least recognition from anyone on the outside. Sober Buddy Matching — more on this below. Resources — load this on a calm Tuesday, not at midnight on a hard Saturday. Live Sessions — real conversations, not polished productions.

The live sessions are where the real weight lives. The people showing up to speak are not just Tony. They are people who lived their entire 20s buried in the bottle — in the fun way, the everyone-loves-them-at-parties way — who are now in their 30s and 40s, working out what it quietly cost them. Coming to tell you the truth about what they wish they had known from inside a life where they did not know it. You cannot manufacture that. When it lands, it lands somewhere that stays.

The sober buddy match is the single most effective thing in the entire community. Not the modules. Not the live sessions. The buddy. One person on the same week as you. One check-in per week. The minimum ask, the highest return. Post in the matching channel. Do it now. Do not leave it sitting there.
